Digital Nomad Trends in North America

This project explores the growth trends of remote workers and digital nomads from 2019 to 2024, analyzing how each group has evolved over time. As technology advances and work flexibility increases, more people have embraced remote work, with some choosing a stable home base while others adopt a location-independent lifestyle as digital nomads. By comparing the number of remote workers to digital nomads over the years, this project highlights key trends and insights into the changing nature of work. Digital nomads choose to combine working remotely and traveling for various reasons and lengths of time. This may be for years or shorter “workcations” lasting from weeks to months. Some travel the globe, but many never cross borders, choosing to live and work in a single country or city. They are united by their passion for travel, desire for adventure, and interest in new cultures. While the rate of increase for digital nomads has leveled from its pandemic-induced skyrocketing during 2020 and 2021, the total number of digital nomads has still grown more than 147% since 2019, now reaching 11% of the U.S. workforce overall.

Remote Workers vs. Digital Nomads

From 2019 to 2024, the number of remote workers consistently remained higher than the number of digital nomads. Remote workers saw significant growth from 3.2 million in 2019 to a peak of 11.1 million in 2022 before slightly declining to 10.2 million in 2024. In contrast, digital nomads experienced steady but slower growth, increasing from 4.1 million in 2019 to 7.9 million in 2024. While both groups expanded over the years, remote workers outnumbered digital nomads throughout the period, showing a more substantial initial rise and stabilization in later years.

Age Distrubtion of Digital Nomads

Consistent with prior year study results, younger generations, Gen Z (26%) and Millennials (38%), continue to comprise most digital nomads (64%). The rapid increase in Gen Z digital nomads (born 1997-2012) is the primary driver. In 2019, the oldest Gen Zers were just 22, and very few were digital nomads. In fact, Gen Z’s 2019 share of digital nomads was less than 1%. But over the past five years, Gen Z has aged into the workforce and proven to be an active travel generation. Because of this, in 2024 about one in four digital nomads are Gen Zers. We expect Gen Z’s share of digital nomads to continue to increase. Gen Z is still aging into the workforce, and as they age, they will have more income and greater work opportunities to become digital nomads. Older cohorts’— Gen Xers (born 1965-1980) and Baby Boomers (born 1946- 1964)— share decreased in 2024. The main reason for the decline is that aging Baby Boomers are leaving the workforce, and while many are still traveling, not working while traveling means they no longer meet our digital nomad definition.

Full Time vs Part Time

Education Level

Full-Time vs. Part-Time Employment: Because digital nomads are a mix of full-timers (64%) and part-timers (36%), and many only do it for part of a year, their income varies widely. Although digital nomads report high satisfaction levels, being nomadic is not without challenges.

Education Level: Digital nomads are also, on average, well-educated, with 52% having a college degree or higher (versus 35% for adult Americans) and 18% reporting having an advanced degree (versus 13% for adult Americans).

Most Common Challenges

The top challenges reported by current digital nomads are financial stress (27%), being away from family and friends (26%), personal safety (24%), time zone differences making work difficult (23%), and travel burnout (21%). These challenges are consistent with prior years. However, the share of digital nomads reporting these challenges has declined since 2020, primarily due to the loosening of the pandemic and pandemic restrictions.

Industries That Digital Nomads Work In

Digital nomads work in a wide variety of fields, with the main professions including information technology (19%), creative services (14%); education and training (9%); sales, marketing, and PR (9%); finance and accounting (8%); and consulting, coaching, and research (7%).

In conclusion, explored how remote workers consistently outnumbered digital nomads, with remote work seeing rapid growth before stabilizing, while digital nomadism experienced steady but slower increases. Additionally, the age distribution of digital nomads, revealing that younger professionals and mid-career workers make up the majority of this group. The breakdown of full-time versus part-time digital nomads showed that while many embrace this lifestyle full-time, a significant portion balances it with part-time or freelance work. The education levels within the digital nomad community, noting that most individuals hold at least a bachelor's degree, often in fields that support remote work. Common challenges faced by digital nomads include being away from family, financial stress, and time zone issues. The most popular industries among digital nomads, with technology, marketing, consulting, and creative fields being the most prominent. Overall, this project highlights the evolving nature of work and the increasing flexibility that allows professionals to work remotely, whether from a fixed location or while traveling the world as digital nomads.
